Methodology Guide

Frequentist Z-Test for Proportions

The frequentist approach measures the strength of evidence against a Null Hypothesis ($H_0$), which assumes that Group A (Control) and Group B (Variant) have identical true conversion rates.

Standard Normal Distribution & Z-Score
Shaded red areas represent critical rejection regions ($Z < -Z_{crit}$ or $Z > Z_{crit}$). The blue vertical line is the active test Z-statistic.
Symbol Definition
$N_A, N_B$ Total users/sample sizes per variant
$p_A, p_B$ Observed conversion rates ($\frac{Conversions}{Users}$)
$p_{pooled}$ Overall combined conversion rate across groups
$\alpha$ Significance level (Type I error threshold, e.g. 5%)
Z-Score (Standard Score)

Measures how many standard deviations the observed difference in conversion rates ($p_B - p_A$) lies away from the expected difference of $0$.

$$Z = \frac{p_B - p_A}{\sqrt{p_{pooled}(1-p_{pooled})\left(\frac{1}{N_A} + \frac{1}{N_B}\right)}}$$
P-Value

The probability of observing a difference as large as (or larger than) the one observed, assuming $H_0$ is true. If $p \le \alpha$, we reject the null hypothesis and declare the result **statistically significant**.

Wald Confidence Interval

A range of plausible values for the true absolute lift. A $95\%$ Confidence Interval means that if we repeated the experiment multiple times, $95\%$ of computed intervals would contain the true difference.

$$CI = (p_B - p_A) \pm Z_{crit} \times \sqrt{\frac{p_A(1-p_A)}{N_A} + \frac{p_B(1-p_B)}{N_B}}$$

Bayesian Beta-Binomial Model

The Bayesian approach models the conversion rates of variants A and B as probability distributions based on observed data.

Live Posterior Density Functions (PDF)
Probability distributions for Control A (cyan) and Treatment B (purple). Higher overlap means higher uncertainty between variants.
Symbol Definition
$Beta(\alpha, \beta)$ Probability distribution for the conversion rate probability
$\alpha_{prior}, \beta_{prior}$ Starting parameters representing uniform prior belief (e.g. $Beta(1,1)$)
$S, F$ Observed Successes (conversions) and Failures (non-conversions)
Posterior Update

Since the Beta distribution is conjugate to the Binomial likelihood, we update beliefs directly by adding successes and failures:

$$\text{Posterior} = Beta(\alpha_{prior} + S, \beta_{prior} + F)$$
Probability $P(\theta_B > \theta_A)$

We draw $100,000+$ random samples from both posterior distributions ($p_A$ and $p_B$) and calculate the proportion of samples where $p_B > p_A$. This yields the direct probability that Variant B is better than Control A.

Expected Loss (Risk)

Measures the average conversion rate percentage points we expect to lose if we deploy a variant and it turns out to be worse. It helps make safe choices, even when results are not yet significant.

$$\text{Loss}_A = E[\max(p_B - p_A, 0)]$$

Multi-Armed Bandit Traffic Allocation

Unlike static A/B tests that route $50\%$ of traffic to a lower-performing variant throughout the test, bandits adjust traffic dynamically to maximize total conversions during the test.

Thompson Sampling Reinforcement Loop
Active cyclical process of user arrivals, posterior drawing, traffic assignment, conversion observation, and posterior update.
Thompson Sampling

For every customer arrival, the bandit draws a conversion rate sample for each arm from its current posterior Beta distribution. The user is assigned to the arm that yielded the highest drawn sample. Successful conversions increase that arm's posterior $\alpha$, while failures increase $\beta$, automatically routing more subsequent traffic to the superior variation.

Cumulative Regret

Regret is the difference between the expected rewards (conversions) obtained by always choosing the optimal variation and the rewards actually obtained under the bandit allocation strategy.

$$\text{Regret} = (p_{\text{best}} \times T) - \sum_{t=1}^T p_{\text{chosen}_t}$$

Thompson Sampling guarantees that cumulative regret grows logarithmically rather than linearly over time.
